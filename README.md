My biggest hurdle was creating the Rainsford_Full_Text.txt file. At first I wanted to use a URL linked to a website from a publicly archived version of the book. This was supposedly the easiest option, as I would not have to download a massive 300 page book nor subject those who wished to run this code to downloading and processing a large document. However, I ran into an issue in the way the text was structured. While I attempted to create a text ratio so that spaCy would only tokenize the main text, I was unable to find the perfect ratio which would meet this target area. My code continuously included parts of the appendix and publisher introduction, all which skewed the actual number of times gender-related terms to appear higher than it truly was. In order to structure my dataset correctly, I instead opted to create my own text file of  "An Historical Account of the Black Empire of Hayti.” In this, I manually removed all text unnecessary to my project’s goal. Thus, creating a repository of only gendered-term in the original body of text written by Rainsford. Based on the preliminary run, I do believe the data supports my initial hypothesis. My original hypothesis was that women will be mentioned in virtue of their relationship to men. I made the claim that the most common female-gendered terms in Rainsford’s text will be “mother”, “wife”, and “daughter”. Based on preliminary results, it does indeed appear that women are mentioned in Rainsford’s text based on their relation to men or status as mothers instead of just being called as they are: “women”. This is unlike the male-related terms table, which saw that men were referred to gendered-words such as “men” and “man”. In fact, only 15 of male-related terms contained reference to being a "father(s)" and 5 mentions to being a "husband(s)" out of approximately 200 tokens. I was surprised to see men mentioned so very few times as husbands and as fathers, especially as I know Rainsford is attributed to humanizing Haitian revolutionists who were often demonized in other colonial publications. A common way to humanize individuals is through narration of being a “family man”. I was also surprised that “daughter” was not as high up in the female-related terms index as “son” was. Looking at these preliminary results, I wonder if these results are in virtue of the language of the Enlightenment period. During this era, phrases like “All men are created free and equal” were rising in popularity, especially during the Haitian Revolution which was fought in the name of black independence, in other words, freeing black men. This is a language quirk I will be keeping in mind as I continue to work on this dataset and further explore how language from Rainsford's text impacted narratives surrounding women and their implied historical significance being tied to their relation to men.
